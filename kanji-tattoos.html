Kanji for Tattoos

A kanji tattoo is permanent, so it's worth spending ten minutes learning what you're actually putting on your skin. Below are kanji that get chosen often, the meaning behind each one, and the mistakes that turn a meaningful tattoo into a confusing one. Read the mistakes section before you book the appointment, not after.

Popular Kanji Choices

愛 (ai)
"Love"
The most requested kanji tattoo in the West. It reads a little bare on its own in Japanese, so many native speakers pair it with a second character like 心 (heart) or 永遠 (eternity) for context.
夢 (yume)
"Dream"
Means an aspiration or goal, the same sense as "follow your dream" in English, not a dream you had last night.
力 (chikara)
"Power / Strength"
A simple, single-stroke-heavy character that reads clearly on its own, which is why it's one of the safer single-kanji choices.
心 (kokoro)
"Heart / Spirit / Mind"
Broader than the English word "heart." It points to inner spirit or will, not the physical organ.
龍 (ryuu)
"Dragon"
The traditional, more detailed form seen in classic art. 竜 is the modern simplified version of the same word.
侍 (samurai)
"Samurai"
Refers specifically to the historical warrior class, not "warrior" or "fighter" in a general sense.
誠 (makoto)
"Sincerity / Truth"
A common name-kanji in Japan, read as a virtue meaning honesty toward yourself and others.
忍 (nin)
"Perseverance / Endurance"
Also the root of "ninja" (忍者). The core meaning is to endure hardship quietly, not stealth itself.
絆 (kizuna)
"Bond"
The emotional tie between people. It became a widely recognized word in Japan after the 2011 earthquake as a symbol of connection during hardship.
平和 (heiwa)
"Peace"
A two-character compound. Using just 和 alone shifts the meaning toward "harmony" instead of "peace."
自由 (jiyuu)
"Freedom"
Always written as the two-character pair, never as a single character on its own.
勇気 (yuuki)
"Courage"
Another two-character compound word, common as a given name element in Japan.
桜 (sakura)
"Cherry Blossom"
Tied culturally to the beauty of things that don't last, since the blossoms fall within about a week of blooming.
空 (sora)
"Sky"
The same character is also read "kuu" in Buddhist contexts, meaning "emptiness" or "void." Which reading applies depends entirely on context, so this one is easy to mistranslate.
運命 (unmei)
"Destiny / Fate"
A compound word describing a life path shaped by forces beyond your control.

Mistakes That Ruin a Tattoo

Generator gibberish
Mistake
Many online "kanji name translator" tools pick characters based on matching English sounds, not meaning. The result often reads as a random string of unrelated words to a native speaker.
Chinese Hanzi vs. Japanese Kanji
Mistake
Japan and China share many characters, but the same character can carry a different, unrelated, or even opposite meaning in each language. Confirm which language's meaning you actually want.
Word-for-word phrase translation
Mistake
Long English sentences translated literally, word by word, usually come out grammatically broken or meaningless once converted to Japanese word order.
A character that doesn't stand alone
Mistake
Some kanji almost never appear by themselves in real Japanese writing. Alone, they can look incomplete or simply strange to a native reader.
Mirrored flash art
Mistake
Generic tattoo flash sheets are sometimes printed as mirror images of the original design, which can flip or distort the character so it no longer reads correctly.
Picking the wrong homophone
Mistake
Japanese has many words that sound identical but use completely different kanji. Choosing the wrong one for the sound you have in mind changes the meaning entirely.

How to Double-Check Before It's Permanent

  1. Ask a native Japanese speaker directly, ideally more than one person, before booking the appointment.
  2. Look the kanji up in a real dictionary, such as Jisho.org, and read the example sentences to see how it's actually used.
  3. Avoid "translator" tools built for names or phrases; they optimize for sound, not meaning.
  4. Bring your own printed reference to the tattoo artist instead of trusting a generic flash-sheet font.
